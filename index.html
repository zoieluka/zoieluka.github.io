2. Introduction

The United States experiences thousands of earthquakes every year, from small tremors to major events capable of causing significant damage. Understanding where earthquakes happen, how deep they occur, and what predicts their magnitude is important for hazard planning and public safety.

In this project, we analyzed a decade of earthquake data from the USGS catalog to answer three questions:

  • Where are earthquakes most concentrated in the contiguous U.S.?
  • Is there a relationship between focal depth and magnitude?
  • Can we predict whether an earthquake will be significant (M ≥ 4.5) using observable features?

3. Data Curation

We used the USGS ANSS Comprehensive Earthquake Catalog (ComCat), queried via the FDSN Event API. We pulled all M3.0+ events in the contiguous U.S. from 2015 to 2025.

4. Exploratory Data Analysis

Finding 1 – Magnitude distribution

Earthquake magnitudes follow a right-skewed distribution consistent with the Gutenberg-Richter law. About 80% of events fall between M3.0 and M4.5. We visualized this with a histogram and KDE overlay.

Finding 2 – California is seismically distinct

A two-sample t-test comparing California's mean magnitude to the rest of CONUS showed a statistically significant difference (p < 0.05), driven by the high concentration of M4–M6 events along the San Andreas fault.

Finding 3 – Depth correlates with magnitude

A Pearson correlation test between depth and magnitude returned r ≈ 0.15 (p < 0.001) — a weak but significant positive relationship. Deeper events tend to be slightly larger, which motivated including depth as a feature in our models.

5. Primary Analysis – Machine Learning

We framed this as a binary classification problem: predict whether an event is significant (M ≥ 4.5) or minor. We trained four classifiers on an 80/20 stratified train-test split:

  • Logistic Regression (baseline)
  • Decision Tree
  • Random Forest (primary model)
  • K-Nearest Neighbors

Features used: latitude, longitude, depth, year, month, hour, dayofweek.

Random Forest performed best. Latitude and longitude were the most important features, confirming that geography is the strongest predictor of earthquake significance.

7. Insights and Conclusions

Geography is the strongest predictor of earthquake significance — where an earthquake happens matters more than when or how deep. Depth adds modest but real predictive value. Temporal features contributed less than expected, and no clear seasonal pattern emerged.

Class imbalance (only ~15% of events are M4.5+) meant we evaluated models using AUC and recall rather than raw accuracy. Both metrics were meaningfully above random baseline.

Future work could incorporate fault proximity data, induced seismicity factors (like wastewater injection), or frame this as a regression problem to predict continuous magnitude.
